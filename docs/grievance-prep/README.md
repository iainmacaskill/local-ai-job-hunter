# Workplace grievance & exit preparation pack

> **Important:** This pack is general information to help you prepare, not legal
> advice. John (your union rep) and, before signing anything, an independent
> solicitor are the people whose advice governs. Everything here is written to
> be checked with them.

## Your stated goal

You do not want an Employment Tribunal claim. You want a **dignified exit** —
leaving on agreed terms, with your health, finances and reference protected.

That is a realistic and common outcome, and it is usually reached through a
**settlement agreement** (a legally binding deal where you leave on agreed
terms and waive claims in exchange for a payment and other protections).

## How the pieces fit together

Not wanting a tribunal does **not** mean the tribunal position is irrelevant.
Your negotiating strength for a good exit package comes almost entirely from
what the employer thinks its exposure is. So the preparation John asked for —
timeline, documents, evidence — serves the exit strategy directly, even though
you never intend to file a claim.

The usual sequence looks like this:

1. **Get the evidence organised** (this pack: `timeline.md` and
   `evidence-checklist.md`). Send it to John.
2. **Tell John your goal is a negotiated exit** (draft in
   `reply-to-john-draft.md`). Union reps negotiate settlements regularly; he
   needs to know this is the destination so he can steer that way.
3. **Raise the grievance anyway** (skeleton in `grievance-letter-draft.md`).
   A well-evidenced grievance is normally what brings the employer to the
   table. It also protects you if negotiation fails — you have not lost
   anything by raising it. The grievance and the exit discussion run as two
   separate tracks: the grievance is "open" correspondence; the exit talk
   happens "without prejudice" / as a protected conversation.
4. **Negotiate the exit** (what to ask for is in `exit-negotiation-notes.md`).
5. **Sign nothing without independent legal advice.** A settlement agreement
   is only binding if you have had advice from an independent adviser
   (usually a solicitor — often arranged via the union), and the employer
   normally pays a contribution towards that advice.

## Rules to hold onto in the meantime

- **Do not resign.** Resigning before a deal is agreed throws away almost all
  of your leverage and can affect benefits. A dignified exit is one you
  negotiate *from* employment, not after leaving it.
- **Follow your GP's advice.** You are signed off; stay signed off until you
  are fit. Keep fit notes continuous with no gaps.
- **Keep everything in writing** where possible, and keep copies at home
  (personal email or printed) — not only on work systems you could lose
  access to.
- **Don't discuss the situation on work channels** or with colleagues beyond
  what is necessary; assume anything on work systems can be read.
- **Route everything through John.** If the employer contacts you directly
  about meetings, you are entitled to be accompanied by your union rep at
  grievance and disciplinary hearings, and it is reasonable to ask for dates
  that allow him to attend.
