# Notes on negotiating a dignified exit

General information for discussion with John and, before signing anything,
an independent solicitor. UK employment context.

## What a "dignified exit" usually is

A **settlement agreement**: a written, legally binding agreement in which you
leave employment on agreed terms and waive the right to bring specified
claims, in exchange for a payment and other protections.

Key facts:

- It is **only binding if you receive independent legal advice** on it from a
  qualified adviser (usually a solicitor; unions often arrange this). The
  employer customarily pays a contribution toward that advice (commonly a few
  hundred pounds — ask for it to be covered in full).
- The **first £30,000 of a genuine ex-gratia termination payment is normally
  tax-free**. Notice pay and holiday pay are taxed as usual. The solicitor
  will check the tax treatment.
- Discussions happen **"without prejudice"** (or as a "protected
  conversation") so neither side can rely on them in open proceedings. Note —
  and John will know this — the statutory "protected conversation" regime
  does **not** cover discrimination complaints, which quietly strengthens
  your side of the table given the harassment allegations.

## What to ask for (the checklist for John)

Money:

- [ ] **Notice pay** — full notice period, paid (worked, on garden leave, or
      as a payment in lieu)
- [ ] **Ex-gratia payment** — the negotiable core. Reference points: months of
      salary, what a tribunal award might look like (injury to feelings for
      harassment is a recognised head of loss — the *Vento* bands — even
      though you don't intend to claim), length of service, and how long a
      job search might take
- [ ] **Accrued untaken holiday** paid in full
- [ ] **Sick pay** — continue receiving contractual/statutory sick pay until
      the termination date; don't let the termination date cut it short
- [ ] **Pension contributions** up to the termination date
- [ ] **Legal fees contribution** — employer pays for the independent advice
- [ ] Any **bonus/commission** accrued

Dignity and future protection:

- [ ] **Agreed reference** — wording annexed to the agreement, plus a
      commitment that verbal references match it
- [ ] **Agreed internal/external announcement** of your departure
- [ ] **Mutual** confidentiality — binding them as well as you
- [ ] **Mutual non-derogatory** clause
- [ ] **No admission-of-liability wording is normal** — don't be put off by it
- [ ] **Resignation vs termination** framing — agree how the departure is
      described (e.g. "mutual agreement")
- [ ] **Termination date** that works for you (benefits, job search, sick pay)

Standard carve-outs the solicitor will insist on (waiver must NOT cover):

- [ ] Accrued **pension rights**
- [ ] **Personal injury** claims you don't yet know about
- [ ] Enforcing the settlement agreement itself

Things to watch:

- [ ] **Confidentiality clauses cannot lawfully stop you** reporting crimes to
      the police, making protected disclosures (e.g. the safeguarding and
      fire-safety concerns), or speaking to regulators, medical professionals
      or the union. The solicitor should check the clause carefully — this
      matters in a case involving harassment and safety concerns.
- [ ] **New job clause** — some agreements reduce payments if you start work
      before a date; resist or narrow this.
- [ ] Don't let the process be rushed — you're entitled to a reasonable
      period (ACAS suggests a minimum of 10 calendar days) to consider a
      settlement offer.

## Negotiating posture

- Your leverage = the strength of the grievance file + the employer's cost
  and risk of the alternative (a contested grievance, possible whistleblowing
  and discrimination exposure, management time, and an unwell employee on
  long-term sick with an open-ended absence). You never have to threaten a
  tribunal for the employer to price that in — they will do it themselves
  once the grievance lands.
- **First offers are usually low.** Treat the first number as an opening, not
  a verdict.
- **Silence is fine.** You are signed off sick; there is no deadline pressure
  on you. The employer carries the open grievance and the ongoing absence.
- If negotiation stalls or fails, you have lost nothing: the grievance
  proceeds, the sickness process proceeds, and you decide next steps with
  John then. (If a tribunal ever did become relevant, remember ACAS Early
  Conciliation is a required first step and time limits are strict — but that
  is a bridge for later, if ever.)

## What NOT to do

- Do **not** resign before terms are agreed and signed.
- Do **not** sign anything, or verbally accept any figure, before the
  independent solicitor has reviewed it.
- Do **not** discuss settlement directly with the employer — route it through
  John.
- Do **not** return to work against GP advice as a bargaining gesture.
